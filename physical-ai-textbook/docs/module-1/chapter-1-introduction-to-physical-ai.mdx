# Chapter 1: Introduction to Physical AI

## Learning Objectives

- Define Physical AI and embodied intelligence
- Understand the limitations of purely digital AI
- Explain why humanoid robots matter in human environments

## Conceptual Foundations

Physical AI represents the integration of artificial intelligence with physical systems, enabling machines to interact with the real world. Unlike traditional digital AI that operates in virtual environments, Physical AI systems must navigate the complexities of physics, sensor noise, actuator limitations, and real-time constraints (Rus & Tolley, 2015).

### What is Physical AI?

Physical AI refers to AI systems that are embodied in physical form and interact directly with the physical world. These systems must account for:

- Physical laws (gravity, friction, momentum)
- Sensor limitations and noise
- Actuator constraints and delays
- Real-time processing requirements
- Safety considerations

### Embodied Intelligence

Embodied intelligence is the concept that intelligence emerges from the interaction between an agent and its environment (Brooks, 1991). Key aspects include:

- The body as a computational resource (Pfeifer & Bongard, 2006)
- Environmental affordances for cognition
- Morphological computation
- Situated action and perception (Lungarella & Bongard, 2006)

## System Architecture

The architecture of a Physical AI system typically includes (Chen & Wang, 2017):

1. **Perception Layer**: Sensors that gather information about the environment
2. **Cognition Layer**: AI algorithms that process information and make decisions
3. **Action Layer**: Actuators that execute physical actions
4. **Control Layer**: Middleware that coordinates perception, cognition, and action

### Data Flow in Physical AI Systems

```
Environment → Sensors → Perception → Cognition → Action → Actuators → Environment
     ↑                                    ↓
     └─────────── Feedback Loop ───────────┘
```
